Ask any counsellor what the single most common complaint is in a struggling marriage, and you will hear some version of the same sentence: "We just don't talk anymore, not really." Not the small talk about the children's school fees or whose turn it is to cook. Real talk. The kind that builds understanding instead of distance.

Communication is not a soft skill you either have or don't; it is a set of habits. And like any habit, it can be unlearned and relearned. Here are five that consistently transform the marriages I work with.

## 1. Speak to Be Understood, Not to Win

Many couples argue like lawyers building a case, each one gathering evidence to prove they are right and their spouse is wrong. But marriage is not a courtroom, and there is no prize for winning an argument against the person you have covenanted to build a life with.

The shift is small but powerful: before you speak, ask yourself, "Am I trying to help my spouse understand me, or am I trying to defeat them?" Communication built on winning always leaves a loser in the room with you, and that loser is usually the marriage itself.

## 2. Practice Reflective Listening

Most of us listen to reply, not to understand. While our spouse is still speaking, we are already rehearsing our rebuttal. Reflective listening interrupts this pattern: after your spouse speaks, you repeat back what you heard, in your own words, before responding.

> "You cannot truly respond to what you have not truly heard. Reflective listening slows the conversation down just enough for real understanding to happen."

This single habit, practiced consistently, resolves more conflict than any clever argument ever could.

### A Simple Framework to Try Tonight

- Let your spouse finish speaking completely before you respond
- Say, "What I hear you saying is…" and reflect it back
- Ask, "Did I understand that correctly?" before moving forward
- Only then, share your own perspective

## 3. Address Issues Early, Not After They Compound

Silence feels peaceful in the short term, but unspoken frustrations do not disappear; they compound with interest. A small irritation ignored today becomes a resentment in six months, and an emotional landmine in two years.

Healthy couples develop the courage to raise small issues while they are still small, calmly and without accusation. This requires safety; both partners need to know that raising a concern will not trigger punishment or withdrawal.

## 4. Separate the Person from the Problem

"You never help around the house" attacks a person's character. "I'm feeling overwhelmed with the housework this week and could use your help" addresses a problem. The difference in how each lands is enormous.

This is not about softening the truth; it is about aiming your words at the actual issue instead of at your spouse's identity. Couples who master this distinction can disagree strongly without the disagreement threatening the relationship itself.

## 5. Make Time for Undistracted Conversation

You cannot build deep communication in the gaps between phone notifications. Every healthy marriage I have coached has, at some point, deliberately carved out undistracted time, even just fifteen minutes a day, where phones are away and full attention is given.

## The Faith Dimension of Listening

Scripture tells us to be "quick to listen, slow to speak, slow to become angry" (James 1:19). This is not merely good advice, it is a spiritual discipline. Choosing to listen well to your spouse is, in a very real sense, an act of honoring God's design for marriage as a covenant of mutual care and humility.

None of these habits are complicated, but none of them are easy either, especially in the middle of an argument. That is exactly why coaching and counselling help: an outside voice can interrupt old patterns in real time and help you practice new ones until they become second nature.

If communication has become a source of pain rather than connection in your marriage, you do not have to figure it out alone. Reach out; a few guided sessions can change the entire trajectory of how you and your spouse talk to each other.
