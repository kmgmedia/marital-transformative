"I forgive you" are three of the easiest words to say and three of the hardest to actually mean. I have sat with countless couples who have said this sentence to each other and yet, months or years later, the same wound resurfaces in every argument — proof that something in the forgiveness process never actually completed.

Forgiveness in marriage is not a feeling that washes over you when enough time has passed. It is a decision, made deliberately and often repeatedly, to release a debt that your spouse cannot fully repay.

## What Forgiveness Is Not

Before we talk about what real forgiveness looks like, it helps to clear away the misconceptions that keep couples stuck.

Forgiveness is not forgetting. Pretending an offense never happened does not heal it — it buries it, and buried wounds have a way of resurfacing at the worst possible moments.

Forgiveness is not condoning. Choosing to forgive your spouse does not mean the offense was acceptable, minor, or excusable. You can fully forgive something you also fully acknowledge was wrong.

Forgiveness is not the same as trust or reconciliation. You can forgive someone and still need time — sometimes a great deal of time — before trust is rebuilt. Forgiveness releases the debt; rebuilding trust is a separate, ongoing process built through consistent, changed behaviour.

## The Decision Beneath the Feeling

> "Forgiveness does not begin with a feeling of readiness. It begins with a decision made before the feeling arrives — and the feeling follows, often slowly, in the decision's wake."

Many spouses wait to forgive until they *feel* like forgiving. But that feeling may never arrive on its own; anger and hurt are not defeated by patience alone; they are defeated by a decision, renewed daily, to release what you are owed.

This does not mean suppressing your pain or rushing past it. Genuine forgiveness usually follows a season of honest grief — you cannot release a debt you have not first fully acknowledged.

## A Practical Path to Forgiving Well

### Name the Debt Clearly

Vague hurt is hard to release. Be specific, at least in your own reflection or with a counsellor, about exactly what was broken — a promise, a boundary, a sense of safety — so that what you are forgiving is clear rather than a fog of general resentment.

### Separate the Decision from the Emotion

Make the decision to forgive even before the emotional sting has fully faded. Say the words honestly to God and, when ready, to your spouse — understanding that emotional healing may still take time to catch up with the decision you have made.

### Release the Right to Punish

Perhaps the clearest sign forgiveness has genuinely taken root is this: you stop using the offense as ammunition in unrelated arguments. If old wounds keep resurfacing in every disagreement, forgiveness has been declared but not yet completed.

## The Faith Foundation for Forgiveness

Scripture ties our capacity to forgive others directly to having received forgiveness ourselves: "forgive one another, as God in Christ forgave you" (Ephesians 4:32). This is not a guilt-trip — it is a genuine wellspring. Couples who ground their forgiveness in an experience of being forgiven themselves tend to forgive far more freely than those relying purely on willpower.

Forgiveness, understood this way, is not a sign of weakness or naivety. It is one of the most courageous acts available to a married couple — a refusal to let the worst moment of your marriage define its entire future.

If you and your spouse are struggling to move past a specific hurt, you do not have to navigate the forgiveness process alone. A skilled counsellor can help you name what was broken, process the grief honestly, and walk the path toward genuine release — at a pace that respects both the depth of the wound and the marriage you are fighting to keep.
